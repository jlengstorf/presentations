  • MY FREAKIN’ BEARD FELL OUT
I believe I had something called alopecia barbae, which causes unexplained loss of whiskers in beards. There’s no proven cause, but some research suggests stress can create symptoms. I’m an n=1 study, but I am now extremely convinced that this is true.

I was already burning out.

My decision:

Poor & healthy

I cut back on everything:

  • I only accepted calls during business hours
  • I turned off ALL notifications
  • I turned on Do Not Disturb mode from 6pm to 8am
  • I only checked email twice a day
  • I set up activities with my friends...
  • ...and left my phone in the car while I was there

I was ready to lose business if I could have my life back

…but that’s not what happened

5 Strategies to
Boost Productivity

  1. Track your time (passively)
  2. Time-box tasks
  3. Front-load important work
  4. Prioritize aggressively
  5. DO NOT multitask

Strategy #1:

Track Your Time

The simple act of acknowledging how we spend our time encourages us to use it more wisely.

If I have a long day...

...I know to take a day off

Strategy #2:

Time-Box Tasks

You’re never more productive than when you’re up against a deadline with 13% battery left.

Find your rhythm.

  • Decide how long you want to focus for
  • Close and turn off everything unrelated
  • Start a timer
  • Unleash your brilliance upon the world
  • When the timer goes off, take a break
  • Repeat
Experiment with different periods of time to figure out what’s best for your attention span and comfort. I find that 90 minutes works well, but my partner does best with 60-minute blocks. Others swear by the 25-minute Pomodoro method — everyone will have their own ideal focus period.

After you’ve experimented with this for a while, you’ll likely notice that you’ve finished your todo list in six hours or less.

Why This Works

1. We’re not distracted

Interruptions are expensive — writing code requires creating complex mental models, and if we’re interrupted or distracted, we lose a lot of time rebuilding them to remember what we were trying to accomplish.

2. We get into a flow state

Without interruptions, we can get into what Mihaly Cziksentmihalyi calls a “flow state” — being “in the zone” — which is where we’re deeply engaged in the task at hand. This is where our best (and most satisfying) work happens.

3. We’re taking breaks

However, if we‘re working in a flow state and not taking regular breaks, we hit what I like to call the “code zombie” state: after a few hours, we hit the wall and get into that foggy, not-quite-human state where we can’t really do useful work anymore, but we’re also too mentally fried to go out and interact with other humans. (Marisa calls this “forgetting how to be a person”.)

4. We’re still available

Another critical point here is that by time-boxing, we’re not sacrificing availability for focus. We can check in on our colleagues, clients, and other communication channels every 90 minutes or so, allowing us to stay available without multitasking or waiting until the middle of the night to get “real work” done.

Also: science

We all have something called an “ultradian rhythm”, which regulates our energy in 90-minute-ish intervals.8

Strategy #3:

Front-Load Important Work

Motivation and self-control are resources that deplete as the day goes on. Don’t waste them.

We’re the best versions of ourselves early in our day

  • energy is higher
  • focus is improved
  • self-control is at its peak
This is NOT me saying you should wake up early. Wake up whenever you want; just do the important shit before you do anything else.

Strategy #4:

Prioritize Aggressively

If everything is The Most Important Thing™, nothing is.

Use the Highlander Technique

Put tasks into a tournament bracket...

...and then start choosing.

Strategy #5:

DO NOT Multitask

Ever. Just stop it.

Context Switching is
fatal to productivity

Let’s Recap

If we work too much, we’re:

  • worse at our jobs
  • more likely to burn out
  • less likely to feel satisfied at our job
  • less able to enjoy our lives

If we work a reasonable schedule, we’re:

  • more creative
  • more productive
  • happier (both in and out of work)
  • healthier

We can be more productive in less time by:

  1. paying attention to how we spend our time
  2. time-boxing tasks to create urgency & limit distractions
  3. front-loading demanding tasks while our energy is high
  4. aggressively prioritizing work to enable better focus
  5. never, ever, ever multitasking again

We all have a choice

We can live this life…

…or this one.
